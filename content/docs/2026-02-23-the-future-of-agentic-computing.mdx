Three things are converging that could fundamentally change the future of AI: companies are starting to print model weights directly onto silicon, frontier models are plateauing, and model knowledge is diverging from model ability. The implications ripple through hardware, databases, privacy, and the entire software stack.

I initially got inspired by a [Hacker News discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47103661) and it turned into a deep research rabbit hole. Here's where I landed.

## Printing LLMs on Chips

The idea is deceptively simple: instead of storing a model's weights in memory and reading them during inference, you bake them directly into the chip's wiring. One transistor per weight, permanently etched into silicon. No memory bandwidth bottleneck, no expensive high-bandwidth memory chips, no power-hungry data shuffling. Just raw, hardwired inference.

[Taalas](https://taalas.com) is the most visible company pursuing this approach, claiming [~17,000 tokens/sec on Llama 3.1 8B at ~250W](https://kaitchup.substack.com/p/taalas-hc1-absurdly-fast-per-user) — roughly 50x faster than NVIDIA Blackwell. But [they're not alone](https://www.trendforce.com/presscenter/news/20260120-12887.html). The broader trend toward custom inference ASICs is accelerating across the industry: [Etched](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/ai-chip-startup-etched-raises-160625909.html) ($620M raised), [EnCharge AI](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20250213156276/en/EnCharge-AI-Closes-Oversubscribed-$100M-Series-B-Funding-Round-Led-by-Tiger-Global-to-Produce-Transformative-AI-Accelerator-Solutions-with-Unprecedented-Efficiency-for-Client-Computing) ($144M), [Cerebras](https://techcrunch.com/2026/01/14/openai-signs-deal-reportedly-worth-10-billion-for-compute-from-cerebras/) ($10B+ agreement with OpenAI), [Groq](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/12/24/nvidia-buying-ai-chip-startup-groq-for-about-20-billion-biggest-deal.html) (acquired/licensed by NVIDIA for $20B).

The approach borrows from the [structured ASIC playbook](https://www.eetimes.com/taalas-specializes-to-extremes-for-extraordinary-token-speed/) of the early 2000s: pre-manufacture a base chip with all the common circuitry, then customize just the final wiring layers for a specific model. A compiler-like system generates the chip design in about a week, then the foundry turns around just those layers. This eliminates the biggest bottleneck in LLM inference — the "memory wall" where the chip spends most of its time waiting for model weights to arrive from memory rather than actually computing. The claimed result: 1000x better performance-per-watt and 20x lower build cost than an H100.

The main counterargument is that these chips run exactly one model, permanently. New model versions arrive every few months and new silicon requires a ~2 months turnaround. But this concern is weaker than it looks, for four reasons.

**The 2-month turnaround is bearable.** The base chip — the expensive, slow part — can be stockpiled in advance. Only the final wiring layers are customized per model. [MarkTechPost](https://www.marktechpost.com/2026/02/22/taalas-is-replacing-programmable-gpus-with-hardwired-ai-chips-to-achieve-17000-tokens-per-second-for-ubiquitous-inference/) frames this as a "seasonal hardware cycle" — fine-tune a model in spring, deploy hardwired chips by summer. For the vast majority of production workloads (classification, extraction, summarization, routing, rewriting), a model that was state-of-the-art 8 weeks ago is more than adequate.

**Most use cases don't need frontier models.** One analysis argues [frontier models are overkill for ~80% of enterprise workloads](https://www.cake.ai/blog/why-smaller-models-beat-frontier-ai-for-most-enterprise-workloads) — summarization, extraction, classification, and routing all succeed reliably on much smaller models. [DeepSeek V3.2](https://www.digitalbricks.ai/blog-posts/the-age-of-frontier-intelligence) delivers roughly 80–90% of top model capability at significantly lower operational cost. [Menlo Ventures' 2025 survey](https://menlovc.com/perspective/2025-the-state-of-generative-ai-in-the-enterprise/) confirms enterprise preference for cost-effective models over bleeding-edge ones.

**Models are plateauing and converging.** The Elo gap between the top model and the 10th-ranked model on Chatbot Arena shrank from 11.9% to 5.4% in a single year. On MMLU, the gap between best open-weight and proprietary models collapsed from [17.5 percentage points to ~0.3](https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2025-ai-index-report). [Comprehensive statistical analysis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S092523122503214X) found gains become less consistent at extremely large training scales. Sebastian Raschka's [State of LLMs 2025](https://magazine.sebastianraschka.com/p/state-of-llms-2025) argues much of 2026's progress will come from improved tooling and inference-time scaling rather than fundamental training breakthroughs. DeepSeek R1 [matched OpenAI's o1 at under $6M training cost](https://www.rdworldonline.com/this-week-in-ai-research-a-0-55-m-token-model-rivals-openais-60-flagship/); open-source now [controls over half the LLM market](https://menlovc.com/perspective/2025-mid-year-llm-market-update/) for on-premises solutions.

**Tool-augmented architectures make model freshness irrelevant.** This is the killer argument. A hardwired Llama running at 17,000 tokens/sec that can call search APIs, use RAG, and invoke tools doesn't need to have been trained on yesterday's data. The model provides reasoning and language capability; the tools provide freshness. This decoupling of "intelligence" from "knowledge recency" makes the 2-month lag tolerable.

Bottom line: if frontier models are plateauing and open-weight models keep closing the gap, a hardwired Llama 4 that's 6 months old but runs at 17,000 tok/s might outperform a current frontier model *in practice* for most tasks — because speed enables richer agent loops, more retrieval passes, and better chain-of-thought at negligible marginal cost.

I'd rate "printing LLMs on chips becomes common" at about 5–6/10 for high-volume, latency-sensitive inference workloads where the model is stable and context windows are modest — a genuinely large slice of production AI. Common the way ASICs became common in networking: specialized, high-volume, quietly ubiquitous in the infrastructure layer even if most people never think about them.

### Where Printed Chips Will Dominate

The use cases that fit best are the ones where latency matters, the task is stable, and cloud costs would be prohibitive at scale: audio transcription, real-time translation, limitless-style recording (recording your entire day), object detection for security cameras and robotics, self-driving cars, and agentic workloads that pull in the latest information dynamically. Unless frontier models manage to stay meaningfully ahead of what can be achieved on printed models, printed models will simply be the norm for 90% of use cases.

Scaling to frontier models (70B+) requires multi-chip configurations not yet demonstrated — a DeepSeek R1-671B equivalent would need ~30 separate custom tape-outs. The logistics get heavy but are manageable for high-value deployments.

### What Remains Unproven

I want to be honest about the limitations. Current printed chips have limited on-chip memory for tracking conversation context, meaning they work best for shorter interactions — Hacker News commenters noted they're compelling for sub-10K token contexts but less so for long-context reasoning. [Aggressive weight compression](https://www.ctol.digital/news/taalas-hc1-review-17000-tokens-per-second-219m-raised-five-risks-every-investor-must-know/) (3-bit quantization) causes acknowledged quality loss. Most performance claims remain self-reported with no independent benchmarks. NVIDIA's next-generation Vera Rubin [promises 5x inference improvement](https://wccftech.com/nvidia-rubin-most-advanced-ai-platform-50-pflops-vera-cpu-5x-uplift-vs-blackwell/) over Blackwell, which would narrow the efficiency gap. And there's architecture risk — if the industry shifts away from transformers, chips hardwired for transformers become paperweights. Some approaches mitigate this with faster turnaround times, but [others like Etched's Sohu would take ~3 years to redesign](https://techcrunch.com/2024/06/25/etched-is-building-an-ai-chip-that-only-runs-transformer-models/).

The broader ASIC landscape supports the thesis. [TrendForce projects](https://www.trendforce.com/presscenter/news/20260120-12887.html) custom ASIC shipments growing 44.6% in 2026 versus GPU shipments at 16.1%. [Deloitte forecasts](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/technology-media-and-telecom-predictions/2026/compute-power-ai.html) inference accounting for ~2/3 of all AI computation in 2026.

## Agents Will Get Way Faster and Cheaper

The speed implications alone are staggering. 17,000 tok/s versus 350 tok/s is ~50x faster inference. Models deployed on edge devices eliminate network latency entirely. Every "turn" in tools like Cursor or Claude Code that currently takes seconds could compress to sub-second timelines.

The cost collapse is equally dramatic. Inference costs have already [dropped ~1,000-fold in three years](https://www.piie.com/blogs/realtime-economics/2026/how-ai-boom-shrugged-deepseek-shock-and-keeps-gaining-steam) while demand rose ~10,000-fold — the cost to achieve a benchmark AI task score plunged from $4,500 to $11.64, a 387x improvement. Printed chips push this further with 20x lower build cost than H100 and 1000x better performance-per-watt.

What this enables: richer agent loops with more retrieval passes and better chain-of-thought at negligible marginal cost. Agents running in parallel across cloud, edge, desktop, and mobile. Always-on AI assistants that are economically viable at consumer scale.

## Edge Will Grow Faster Than Cloud

GCP, Azure, and AWS will remain enormous — but edge-oriented and hardware-oriented companies (Cloudflare, Apple, Vercel, PC manufacturers) will grow faster in percentage terms.

[AI PCs will hit 55% of global PC shipments by 2026](https://www.computerworld.com/article/4047019/ai-pcs-to-surge-claiming-over-half-the-market-by-2026.html) (Gartner). Qualcomm's [Snapdragon X2 Elite reaches 80 TOPS](https://www.windowscentral.com/hardware/qualcomm/snapdragon-x2-elite-extreme-announcement-2025). AMD's [Ryzen AI Max runs Llama 3 70B on a laptop](https://spectrum.ieee.org/ai-models-locally). Apple's on-device AI strategy offers inference at zero API cost to developers — potentially the most significant edge play. Dr. Ben Lee (UPenn/Google) estimates we ["could be getting 80% of compute done locally and leaving 20% for the data center cloud"](https://www.latitudemedia.com/news/catalyst-will-inference-move-to-the-edge/).

The counterarguments are real, though. Cloud AI is [$89–122B in 2025](https://www.grandviewresearch.com/industry-analysis/cloud-ai-market-report), growing to [$363B–$1.7T by 2030–2033](https://www.precedenceresearch.com/artificial-intelligence-market); edge AI is [$25–36B in 2025](https://www.mordorintelligence.com/industry-reports/edge-ai-hardware-market). Microsoft alone invested $80B in AI infrastructure in 2025 — more than the entire edge AI market. Hyperscalers are also investing in edge: Azure Arc, AWS Outposts, Google Distributed Cloud. Edge and cloud are complementary, not competitive; hyperscalers are winning both games simultaneously.

But the growth rate differential matters. When printed chips make edge inference 50x faster and 1000x more power-efficient, the economics shift hard toward running things locally whenever you can.

## Jevons's Paradox: AI Use Will Explode

The evidence is unambiguous. DeepSeek R1 delivered comparable performance at ~27x cheaper inference costs — and usage exploded rather than spending declining. Anthropic revenue surged from $1B to $9B run rate. OpenAI expects $20B in 2025 revenue, up from $6B in 2024. [Enterprise AI spending surged 320%](https://menlovc.com/perspective/2025-the-state-of-generative-ai-in-the-enterprise/). The [IEA projects](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/energy-demand-from-ai) global data center electricity doubling to 945 TWh by 2030. A single ChatGPT query uses ~10x the energy of a Google search. Every 10% improvement in AI computing efficiency has historically led to 20–30% increase in overall deployment and usage.

The interesting question with ASICs: if printed chips use 1000x less power per inference, does Jevons's Paradox still hold? Historical precedent says yes — efficiency gains get consumed by demand growth. But ASICs could moderate the energy crisis even as usage explodes. As Satya Nadella put it: ["the biggest issue we are now having is not a compute glut, but it's power... I have a bunch of chips sitting in inventory that I can't plug in."](https://techcrunch.com/2025/11/03/altman-and-nadella-need-more-power-for-ai-but-theyre-not-sure-how-much/)

## Network Latency Becomes the Bottleneck

Here's where it gets interesting for the software stack.

Human browsing: a handful of requests to load a page, then the human thinks and reads, then clicks something. Agentic workloads are fundamentally different — rapid back-and-forth turns, iterative steps, fetching, reasoning loops. The latency caused by network waterfalls adds up *much faster*.

With an ultra-fast model on a printed chip running 50x faster with no network latency and all data on the same device, every "turn" compresses to sub-second timelines. But if the model is fast and the data is far away, you've just moved the bottleneck from compute to network.

This is the forcing function for what comes next.

## Local-First Databases Will Take Off

If agents and their data need to be co-located for maximum speed, multi-master, eventually consistent, local-first databases become essential infrastructure for agentic computing. Agents running in parallel across cloud, edge, and desktop use eventually consistent data to reconcile their state.

The ecosystem is already building toward this. There are [26+ major tools](https://electric-sql.com/docs/reference/alternatives) in the space: Turso, PowerSync, ElectricSQL, CRDT libraries like Automerge and Yjs. Adam Wiggins (Heroku co-founder) compares [local-first to "React in 2013"](https://www.heavybit.com/library/article/local-first-development) — exciting but early. Production deployments exist: Figma's CRDT-inspired multiplayer, Linear's IndexedDB-first architecture, Notion's offline mode. The SQLite renaissance is real — [Turso](https://turso.tech/blog/agentfs), LiteFS, Cloudflare D1, PGlite — with Turso/libSQL positioning aggressively for "database-per-agent" architectures. Ink & Switch [joined UK's ARIA Safeguarded AI Programme](https://www.inkandswitch.com/newsletter/dispatch-013/) to build GAIOS, a local-first AI platform.

The strongest counterargument: LLM inference (2–20 seconds) currently dwarfs database access times, so optimizing from 200ms to 1ms saves less than 1% of total agent response time. [Redis is the top choice](https://redis.io/blog/best-ai-agent-data-storage-2025/) for AI agent data storage with sub-millisecond access. [Salesforce](https://engineering.salesforce.com/scaling-ai-systems-secrets-for-managing-100000-training-and-metadata-requests-per-minute/) reduced agent metadata latency from 400ms to sub-millisecond with simple caching — no paradigm shift required.

But this math changes completely with printed chips. If inference drops from seconds to milliseconds, database latency becomes proportionally much more significant. The counterarguments assume current inference speeds. With printed chips, the latency calculus shifts dramatically.

### The Privacy Dimension

There's a deeper reason local-first matters beyond latency.

AI makes "inferential privacy violations" possible — deducing sensitive information never explicitly disclosed through patterns in seemingly innocuous data. The Markup investigated Microsoft's advertising platform Xandr and found [650,000 audience segments](https://themarkup.org/privacy/2023/06/08/from-heavy-purchasers-of-pregnancy-tests-to-the-depression-prone-we-found-650000-ways-advertisers-label-you) that advertisers could use to target people — labels like "Heavy Purchasers of Pregnancy Tests," depression-prone, specific medical diagnoses, and "Struggling Elders" — all inferred from browsing history, purchase records, and location data. The FTC [brought enforcement actions](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2024/01/ftc-order-prohibits-data-broker-x-mode-social-outlogic-selling-sensitive-location-data) against data brokers selling smartphone GPS data that revealed visits to reproductive health clinics, addiction treatment centers, and places of worship.

[53% of Americans say AI does more to hurt than help people keep their personal information private](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2023/08/28/growing-public-concern-about-the-role-of-artificial-intelligence-in-daily-life/) (Pew). Cisco's 2024 Consumer Privacy Survey found [84% of GenAI users are concerned about data entered into AI tools going public](https://newsroom.cisco.com/c/r/newsroom/en/us/a/y2024/m10/cisco-survey-shows-strong-relationship-between-privacy-awareness-and-trust-in-ai.html). No comprehensive U.S. federal privacy law exists, and the regulatory landscape remains fragmented.

With AI so accessible, hackers can use AI to connect disparate data pieces, create profiles, and fill in missing information — this doesn't even require AGI. Local-first architectures provide a structural answer: keep data on-device, under user control.

## What This All Means Together

The composite vision: ultra-fast, ultra-cheap inference on printed chips → agents that operate at sub-second speeds. Edge deployment → no network latency for the model. Local-first databases → no network latency for the data. Tool-augmented architectures → freshness without model retraining. Privacy by architecture → data stays on-device. A fundamentally different computing paradigm where AI is fast, cheap, private, and everywhere.

The terrifying flip side: sub-second agent turns with local data means autonomous AI systems that iterate at inhuman speed. There are legitimate concerns about what happens when you remove all the friction — latency, cost, network dependencies — that currently acts as a natural brake on autonomous AI. And the privacy threat cuts both ways: local-first protects *your* data, but the same printed-chip technology makes surveillance AI cheaper and more ubiquitous too.

The strongest pattern across all the evidence is that AI is expanding every market it touches rather than replacing existing players. Edge grows alongside cloud, not instead of it. Chat augments visual UI rather than replacing it. This expansion pattern is the meta-prediction underlying everything — and it's the one with the strongest evidence.

## Timeline Realism

Predictions in this space are consistently "directionally correct but temporally optimistic." Historical platform transitions take 10–15 years: mainframe → PC (~20 years), PC → internet (~14 years), internet → mobile (~13 years). Today's incumbents control data, distribution, compute, capital, and talent — [they're building, acquiring, and partnering through the AI transition](https://www.fidelity.com/learning-center/trading-investing/AI-outlook), exactly as they did through mobile and cloud. AI-native startups are [deeply intertwined with the very companies they're supposed to displace](https://www.startuphub.ai/ai-news/ai-video/2025/ais-super-cycle-incumbents-lead-disruption-looms/).

The future isn't cloud *or* edge — it's both, with printed chips making edge inference so fast and cheap that it becomes the default for the majority of workloads. The database layer has to follow: if compute goes local, data must go local too. The privacy imperative reinforces the architectural shift.

We're not replacing the current infrastructure — we're building a parallel one that will quietly become ubiquitous, like ASICs in networking. The open question isn't *whether* this happens, but *how fast* — and whether we're ready for what sub-second autonomous AI loops actually mean.
